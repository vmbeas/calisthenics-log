Strength A — Push + Core
Push-up progression 4 × 8–12

Target: 4 × 8–12 → hit 12 all sets = advance variation

Incline — hands on a surface above hip height. Easiest. Good starting point if standard feels rough on your back.
Standard — hands shoulder-width, body rigid plank. The baseline.
Diamond — hands close together forming a diamond shape. Hits triceps hard.
Archer — one arm extends to the side as you lower, loading the opposite side. Precursor to one-arm push-up.
Pseudo planche — hands turned out and pushed toward hips, leans forward over hands. Brutal anterior deltoid and chest load.

▶ Push-up progressions explained
Pike push-up 3 × 6–10

Target: 3 × 6–10 → progress to elevated pike, then wall HSPU

Hips high in an inverted V, hands shoulder-width. Lower your head toward the floor between your hands — chin past the hands at the bottom. This is your overhead pressing movement. Elevated feet (on a chair or box) increases the load and angle, moving toward a handstand push-up position.

▶ Pike push-up tutorial
Dip 3 × 6–10

Target: 3 × 6–10 → rings dip is the long-term goal

Chair dip — hands on chair seat behind you, legs out. Good starting point.
Parallel bars — full body weight, lower until upper arms are parallel to floor. Keep torso slightly forward for chest engagement, more upright for triceps focus.
Rings dip — same movement but rings force your stabilisers to work overtime. Much harder. Needs hang point.

▶ Dip progression tutorial
L-sit hold 3 × 10–20s

Target: 3 × 10–20s → build to full L with straight legs

Arms locked straight, pressing the floor, hips off the ground. Tuck — both knees pulled to chest. Easier entry point. One-leg tuck — one leg extended forward, one tucked. Full L — both legs extended parallel to floor. Can be done on the floor (harder — no room to drop your hips), parallettes, or rings. The pressing strength and hip flexor endurance this builds transfers to everything.

▶ L-sit progression
Hollow body hold 3 × 20–30s

Target: 3 × 20–30s → arms overhead is the progression end

On your back. Press your lower back into the floor — this is the position you're trying to maintain. Lift shoulders and legs off the floor, forming a shallow "C" shape. Bent knees is easiest. Straight legs raises difficulty significantly — lower your feet closer to the floor to make it harder. Arms overhead is the full movement. Foundation for handstands, muscle-ups, and planche.

▶ Hollow body tutorial
Glute bridge 3 × 12–15

Target: 3 × 12–15 → single-leg, then hip thrust off a surface

Back-safe posterior chain work. Lie on back, knees bent, feet flat. Drive hips up squeezing glutes hard at the top — don't let your lower back hyperextend. Single-leg — extend one leg and bridge on the other. Much harder. Hip thrust — shoulders on a bench/couch, adds a larger range of motion. Good substitute for deadlift while the back recovers.

Strength B — Pull + Hinge
Dead hang 2 × 20–30s warm-up

Warm-up: 2 × 20–30s — spinal decompression

Hang from the bar completely relaxed — don't engage your lats or retract your scapulae. Just hang. This passively decompresses the spine, which is especially valuable with a lower back issue. Grip will be the first thing to fail. That's fine early on — it builds fast.

▶ Dead hang benefits
Pull-up progression 4 × 5–8

Target: 4 × 5–8 → build to full pull-ups, then weighted

Scap pull-ups — arms straight, only move is depressing and retracting the shoulder blades. Teaches the start of the movement.
Inverted row — body under a bar/table, pull chest to bar. Flat feet = easier, elevated = harder.
Negatives — jump to the top position, lower yourself over 3–5 seconds. Best way to build pull-up strength before you can do one.
Full pull-ups — dead hang to chin over bar. Overhand grip (pronated) is hardest; underhand (supinated/chin-up) is slightly easier.

▶ Pull-up progression guide
Australian row 3 × 8–12

Target: 3 × 8–12 → always one step ahead of your pull-up progression slot

Horizontal pull — different movement pattern to the vertical pull-up. Both matter. The key is keeping this slot one variation ahead of whatever you're doing in the pull-up slot so you're never doubling up the same exercise in one session:

Pull-up slot → Row slot
Inverted row flat → Inverted row elevated
Inverted row elevated → Rings row
Negatives → Rings row or rings archer row
Full pull-ups → Rings archer row

Rings row — same movement but rings rotate freely, loading the rear delts and rotator cuff differently. Needs a hang point. Archer row — one arm pulls, the other extends out to the side — serious horizontal pulling strength, precursor to muscle-up.

Band pull-apart 3 × 15

Target: 3 × 15 — increase band tension over time

Hold a resistance band in front of you at shoulder height, hands shoulder-width apart. Pull the band apart horizontally until your arms are in a T, squeezing your shoulder blades together. Slow and controlled — this is a rear delt and external rotator exercise, not a speed drill. Counters all the internal rotation from pressing movements.

▶ Band pull-apart tutorial
Single-leg RDL 3 × 8/side

Target: 3 × 8/side — BW until back is solid, then add load

Romanian Deadlift on one leg. Stand on one leg, slight bend in the knee. Hinge at the hip — torso tips forward, free leg goes back as a counterbalance. Lower until you feel a hamstring stretch, then drive the hip forward to return. Keep your spine neutral the entire time. This is a hip hinge with zero spinal compression — ideal for your back situation.

▶ Single-leg RDL tutorial
Pistol squat progression 3 × 5–8/side

Target: 3 × 5–8/side → full pistol is the long-term goal

Single-leg squat with the free leg extended forward. Requires quad strength, hip flexor flexibility, and ankle mobility simultaneously. Assisted — hold a door frame or TRX strap to offload some weight and help balance. Box pistol — squat down to a box or chair, which limits depth and lets you build strength at the bottom. Full pistol — all the way down, free leg parallel to floor.

▶ Pistol squat progression
Jefferson curl 2 × 5 — BW only

⚠️ BW only until 3 consecutive pain-free weeks. STOP if radiating pain.

Stand on a step or elevated surface. Slowly curl your spine forward vertebra by vertebra — chin to chest, then round through the thoracic spine, then lumbar. Arms hang down holding the weight (none, for now). Go only as far as feels comfortable, then slowly uncurl the same way. Counterintuitive for a back issue, but controlled end-range spinal flexion under low load is well-supported for disc rehab. Never rush this.

McGill Big 3 — finisher
Bird dog 3 × 8/side · 8–10s hold

Suggested: 3 × 8/side, hold 8–10s each rep

On hands and knees, spine neutral. Extend opposite arm and leg simultaneously without rotating your hips or arching your lower back. Hold for the count, return under control. This is a spinal stability drill — slow and deliberate beats fast every time.

▶ Watch tutorial
Dead bug 3 × 8/side

Suggested: 3 × 8/side

Lie on your back, arms straight up, hips and knees at 90°. Lower opposite arm and leg toward the floor without letting your lower back leave the ground — that's the whole game. If your back lifts, your range is too big. Exhale as you lower.

▶ Watch tutorial
Side plank 3 × 20–30s/side

Suggested: 3 × 20–30s/side → progress to hip dips

Side-lying on your forearm, body in a straight line from head to feet. Knee-down is the starting point — no shame, it still works the quadratus lumborum hard. Progress to full, then add hip dips (lower hip toward floor and back up) once full feels easy.

Run details
Z3 run 134–152 bpm · 25–35 min

Target: 25–35 min sustained · progress +5 min/week to 40 min

Aerobic zone — you should be working but not gasping. On your Garmin, 134–152 bpm is the Z3 target. HR governs pace — not speed. If it drifts above 152, slow down. Warm up 5 min easy walk, cool down 5 min. The goal is building your aerobic engine for the ACFT 2-mile run.

Walk details
Z2 walk 117–132 bpm · 30–45 min

Target: 30–45 min · incline preferred · nasal breathing check

Active recovery. Incline treadmill (3–5% grade) or a hilly route. If you can't breathe through your nose comfortably, you're above Z2 — slow down. This isn't a workout, it's aerobic maintenance and recovery. Keeps fat oxidation high without taxing the CNS.

Sprint session
Sprint session Max effort repeats · full recovery

Warm up 8–10 min easy jog · cool down 10 min dynamic stretch

Quality over quantity. Each rep should feel fast — not an 85% cruise. Full recovery between reps means your next rep is as fast as your first. If your splits are degrading badly, you've done enough. The goal is neuromuscular speed and lactate tolerance for the ACFT 2-mile, not just fitness.
